Most small business owners believe they have exactly two choices when it comes to marketing, and both of them are bad.

Option one: hire an agency. Get the professionals, hand it off, stop worrying about it. Except the quote comes back at $3,500 a month before you've spent a dollar on actual ads, the management fees eat 30% or more of your budget, and you realize the whole thing is priced for companies ten times your size. Too expensive.

Option two: do it yourself. Save the money, keep control, learn the platforms. Except you already work sixty hours a week running the business, the platforms change every few months, and the campaigns you set up at midnight are quietly wasting money you'll never get back. Too much time you don't have.

So you bounce between them. You try DIY until it becomes overwhelming, then you price out an agency, get sticker shock, and go back to muddling through yourself. A lot of small businesses spend years stuck in that loop.

Here's what almost nobody tells them: the two options aren't the only two. There's a third path that most owners have never had explained to them — and for a business too small for an agency but too busy to DIY, it's usually the right one.

Why the two familiar options fail small businesses

It's worth being clear about why the traditional choices don't fit, because the reasons point straight at the alternative.

The agency is built for someone else. Full-service agencies are genuinely good, but their cost structure assumes a client with a big budget and complex needs. The retainers, the account teams, the 30%-plus management fees — that overhead makes sense when you're spending six figures a month. On a small business budget, you're paying enterprise prices for enterprise infrastructure you don't need. You're subsidizing a machine built for bigger clients.

DIY is built on a resource you don't have: time. Doing it yourself looks free, but it isn't. It costs the scarcest thing you own. Most small business owners have an hour a day for marketing at most, and modern digital advertising across search, social, and everything else is more than an hour-a-day job to do well. So DIY usually means one of two things: campaigns that are set up and neglected, or a slow bleed of your evenings. Neither is actually free.

Both options ask you to overpay — one in money, one in time.

The third option: a consolidated partner

The third path goes by a few names, but the idea is simple. Instead of an expensive agency or a do-it-yourself scramble, you hand your entire marketing operation to a single consolidated partner that runs every channel for you, from one place, at a cost built for a business your size.

It's not an agency in the traditional, overhead-heavy sense. And it's obviously not DIY. It's a team that does the work an agency would do — plan the strategy, run the ads across search, social, programmatic, email, streaming TV, and more — but stripped of the enterprise pricing, the redundant vendors, and the parts you're currently paying for and not using.

What you get:

  • The results of professional marketing — real specialists running your channels, not you at midnight.
  • Without the agency price tag — no enterprise retainer, and a management fee that's a fraction of the traditional cut.
  • Without the DIY time cost — it's genuinely handled, not handed back to you as "homework."
  • All in one place — every channel run by one team, one report, one point of contact, instead of a pile of separate vendors.

What it costs — and what it saves

This is where the third option stops sounding too good to be true and starts making mathematical sense.

The reason a consolidated partner can charge so much less than an agency isn't that the work is worse. It's that the waste is gone. No enterprise retainer built for bigger clients. No duplicated fees from four separate vendors each taking a cut. No shelf of software you're paying for and barely using. No salary, benefits, and overhead of an in-house hire. Strip all of that redundancy out and the price drops without the quality dropping with it.

Measured against the real, all-in cost of the paths most small businesses are on — a full-service agency, a stack of vendors, or a marketing employee plus tools — a consolidated partner can cut what you spend to get your marketing run by more than 70%. You're not buying less. You're just no longer paying for overhead that was never helping you.

Is the third option right for you?

It won't fit every business. If you're big enough to justify a full in-house team and a lead agency, your needs are different. And if you truly have the time and skill to run everything yourself and enjoy it, more power to you.

But the third option is close to ideal if you recognize yourself here:

  • You need to be on several marketing channels, not just one.
  • You don't have the hours to run them all yourself — and doing so is pulling you away from your actual business.
  • An agency's pricing has made you wince, or walk away.
  • You want professional results without a professional-sized bill.
  • You'd love for the whole thing to just be handled, in one place, by people you can actually reach.

If most of that describes you, you've probably been stuck choosing between two options that were never going to fit — when the one that does has been sitting off to the side the whole time.
